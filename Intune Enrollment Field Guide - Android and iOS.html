Microsoft Intune · Device Enrollment Reference

The Intune Enrollment Field Guide

A complete, beginner-friendly walkthrough of every way an Android or iOS/iPadOS device gets into Microsoft Intune — what each method is, why it exists, what it costs you in prerequisites and licensing, and what it feels like for the IT admin and the end user. Built around one consistent worked example — a fictional health system called Contoso Healthcare — so the differences stay concrete instead of abstract.

00How to use this guide

This isn't a single article to read top to bottom in one sitting — it's a reference you'll dip back into. Here's the shape of it, and how the pieces fit together.

Datasheet cards

Every enrollment method gets the exact same set of fields, in the exact same order — definition, real-world use case, prerequisites, licensing, workflow, ownership model, user experience, management capability, security implications, limitations, pros/cons, common mistakes, best practice. Once you've read one card, you know how to skim all the others.

Status badges

Current means Microsoft, Google, Samsung, or Apple actively recommend it today (mid-2026). Legacy means it still works but is being phased out or superseded. Deprecated means don't build anything new on it — it may already be unsupported for new enrollments.

Ownership badges

BYOD marks methods built for personally-owned devices. Corporate marks methods built for organization-owned hardware. Several methods only make sense for one or the other — that's one of the biggest sources of confusion for newcomers, and it's the very first thing this guide untangles.

A note on currency

Mobile device management moves fast. This guide reflects the state of Microsoft Intune, Android Enterprise, Samsung Knox, and Apple's enterprise platform as of June 2026 — including a significant rename you'll see referenced throughout: Apple Business Manager became Apple Business on April 14, 2026. See Sources & currency at the end for what changed recently and what to re-check periodically.

01Core concepts, in plain English

Every enrollment method card later in this guide leans on these terms. Read this section once, properly, and the rest of the guide gets dramatically easier.

Mobile Device Management MDM
Software that lets your organization remotely configure, secure, and monitor a phone, tablet, or computer — push settings, install apps, enforce a passcode, wipe it if it's lost. Intune is Microsoft's MDM (and MAM) product. Think of MDM as a master key plus a settings panel your IT team can reach into from anywhere, without physically touching the device.
Mobile Application Management MAM
A lighter-weight alternative to full MDM. Instead of managing the whole device, Intune manages just the work apps on it (Outlook, Teams, etc.) — wrapping them in policy so corporate data can be encrypted, backed up, or wiped without ever touching the user's personal photos, texts, or other apps. MDM manages the house. MAM manages one locked room inside the house, leaving everything else untouched.
Android Enterprise
Google's modern framework for managing Android devices at work. It replaced the older, weaker "Device Administrator" API. Every current Android enrollment method in this guide — work profile, fully managed, dedicated — is a flavor of Android Enterprise. Intune talks to Android Enterprise through the Android Management API after your tenant is bound to a managed Google Play account. Android Enterprise is the rulebook Google wrote so that any MDM vendor (Intune, but also others) manages Android devices the same secure, supported way.
Managed Google Play
A special enterprise storefront layered on top of the regular Google Play Store. It's how Intune silently pushes, approves, and updates apps on Android Enterprise devices without the user needing a personal Google account or ever touching the consumer Play Store on the work side.
Work Profile
A walled-off, encrypted container that lives inside an Android device. Work apps, work data, and work notifications live inside it; everything else on the phone stays outside it. Intune can only see and control what's inside the work profile — it has no visibility into personal photos, personal WhatsApp, personal browsing, etc. Picture a locked briefcase that travels around inside someone's regular backpack. IT controls the briefcase. The backpack — and everything else in it — stays the user's business.
Fully Managed
The opposite end of the spectrum from a work profile: the entire device is owned and controlled by the organization, with no separate personal space. Intune sees and can configure the whole device, not just a container inside it. This mode is also called device owner mode in Android's own terminology.
Corporate-Owned, Personally Enabled COPE
A hybrid: the organization owns the device outright (fully managed at the device level), but a work profile is still carved out inside it so the employee can also use it personally. Google's current documentation calls this "work profile on a company-owned device," but COPE is still the term you'll hear constantly in the field, in vendor docs, and on certification exams.
Dedicated Device formerly COSU
A fully managed device locked down to one app or a small set of apps, with no personal use at all. Used for kiosks, scanners, shared point-of-care tablets, or digital signage. Google's older name for this was "corporate-owned, single-use" (COSU) — that term is now deprecated in favor of "dedicated device."
Automated Device Enrollment ADE
Apple's zero-touch enrollment system for iPhone, iPad, and Mac. A device bought through an authorized channel is associated with your organization before it ever ships. The very first time it's powered on and connects to Wi-Fi, it automatically finds your MDM (Intune) and applies your settings — no manual profile installation, no IT technician unboxing it by hand.
Apple Business formerly Apple Business Manager / ABM
Apple's web portal for buying devices and apps in bulk, and the thing that links those devices to Intune via ADE. On April 14, 2026, Apple retired the names "Apple Business Manager," "Apple Business Essentials," and "Apple Business Connect," folding everything into a single rebranded platform called Apple Business. The underlying device-enrollment mechanics (ADE, tokens, Managed Apple Accounts) are unchanged — only the name and some packaging changed. You will still hear "ABM" used constantly out of habit, including in this guide's diagrams, because most documentation and IT teams haven't fully switched over yet.
Apple School Manager ASM
Apple Business's sibling platform, purpose-built for K-12 and higher-education institutions. It uses the exact same ADE mechanics as Apple Business but adds education-specific features (Classroom app, Shared iPad student accounts, rostering). Not directly relevant to a healthcare tenant like HNZ, but Intune's ADE setup steps are identical whether the token comes from Apple Business or Apple School Manager.
Supervision
A heightened management state Apple grants to organization-owned devices, almost always set up via ADE. A supervised device unlocks restrictions and controls that simply don't exist on an unsupervised device — things like silently installing apps without user interaction, blocking AirDrop, or forcing Single App Mode. A device enrolled via Account-Driven User Enrollment (BYOD) is never supervised.
Managed Apple Account formerly Managed Apple ID
A company-owned Apple Account, separate from a person's personal Apple ID, used to sign into Shared iPad or to access iCloud-adjacent services on a managed device. When you federate Entra ID with Apple Business, these can be created automatically and matched to a person's Entra username.
Compliance Policy
A rule Intune checks a device against — minimum OS version, passcode required, device not jailbroken/rooted, encryption on, and so on. A device that fails a compliance check gets flagged "Not compliant" in Intune, which Conditional Access can then act on.
Configuration Profile
A set of settings Intune pushes to a device or group of devices — Wi-Fi credentials, VPN settings, restrictions like "disable camera," certificate deployment, app configuration. Compliance policies check a state; configuration profiles set a state.
Conditional Access
An Entra ID feature that gates access to resources (Exchange Online, SharePoint, Teams, etc.) based on conditions — is the device compliant, is it a known/registered device, is the sign-in coming from a trusted location, is MFA satisfied. Intune's compliance status is one of the most common conditions Conditional Access checks before letting someone in.
Device Enrollment Manager DEM
A special Entra ID account type that can enroll a much larger number of devices than a normal user account is allowed to (the default per-user enrollment cap is low). IT teams use a DEM account to bulk-provision shared or kiosk devices without hitting that ceiling.
Declarative Device Management DDM
Apple's newer device-management protocol, now the default across iOS, iPadOS, and macOS. Instead of Intune constantly polling "are you compliant yet?", the device itself watches for changes and proactively reports its status when something changes. The result is faster compliance reporting and less network chatter. Every current Apple enrollment method in this guide rides on top of DDM by default in 2026.
Enrollment Single Sign-On Enrollment SSO
An Apple feature that lets a user authenticate once during ADE setup and have that authentication carried through to the Company Portal app, Safari, and other apps that need it — instead of typing their password three separate times during setup.

02The ecosystem map

Before any single enrollment method makes sense, it helps to see the whole machine. Intune doesn't enroll devices in isolation — it's the hub between your identity system and two completely separate vendor ecosystems, each with its own portal, its own token, and its own rules.

Fig. 1 — Two vendor ecosystems, one MDM hub. Apple's side runs on Apple Business + ADE tokens; Android's side runs on a Managed Google Play binding, fed by Google's zero-touch portal and/or Samsung's Knox Admin Portal.

Notice the shape: Entra ID is the identity backbone for both platforms, but Apple and Android each bring their own separate trust relationship into Intune — a downloaded token file from Apple Business on one side, an OAuth binding to a managed Google Play account on the other. Samsung's Knox Mobile Enrollment and Google's zero-touch portal aren't competitors to Android Enterprise — they're provisioning accelerants that sit in front of it, automating how a device first finds its way into that Android Enterprise binding. The same logical split exists on Apple's side: Apple Business is the trust relationship; ADE is the zero-touch delivery mechanism riding on top of it.

03Mode vs. method — the distinction that untangles everything

Here's the actual source of most beginner confusion, and it's not your fault: Microsoft, Google, and Samsung's own documentation routinely lists two completely different categories of thing side by side, as if they were peers in the same list. They aren't.

Management mode — the "what"

This is the destination state of the device: what it looks like once enrollment finishes. On Android, the modes are Work Profile (BYOD), Work Profile (corporate-owned / COPE), Fully Managed, and Dedicated. On iOS, the modes are roughly Supervised-via-ADE vs. unsupervised user enrollment. A device has exactly one mode at a time, and modes generally can't be changed in-place — switching means a factory reset.

Provisioning method — the "how"

This is the mechanism used to get the device from out-of-the-box to that destination state. On Android: zero-touch, Knox Mobile Enrollment, QR code, token/afw#setup, NFC tag, or the Company Portal app. On iOS: ADE with Setup Assistant, ADE via Apple Configurator, or direct enrollment through the Company Portal app. Several different methods can often land a device in the same mode.

Why this matters

When the user's original question asked to cover "Android Enterprise Corporate Owned Work Profile," "Corporate Owned Fully Managed with Work Profile (COPE)," and "Zero Touch Enrollment" as if they were three items in the same list, that's exactly the trap: the first two are the same management mode described two different ways, and the third is a provisioning method that could be used to reach several different modes, including that one. This guide splits Section 1 into "Management modes" first, then "Provisioning methods" second, specifically so you stop comparing apples to delivery trucks.

Fig. 2 — Several provisioning methods can land a device in the same management mode. Choose the mode first based on ownership and purpose; choose the method second based on how the device was purchased and who's doing the provisioning.

§1Android device enrollment

Every current Android Enterprise option in Intune is built on the same foundation: a managed Google Play binding, a Device Policy Controller (the Intune app itself acts as this), and one of four management modes. We'll cover the four modes first — the "what" — then the methods used to provision into them — the "how" — then what's been retired along the way.

Management modes (the "what")

Four modes exist today. Picking the right one starts with two questions: who owns the device, and does it need to support personal use. Get those two answers and the mode picks itself.

ANDROID · MANAGEMENT MODE · AE-01

Work Profile — Personally-Owned (BYOD)

Current BYOD

What it is: A walled-off, encrypted container created inside an employee's own Android phone. Work apps, work data, and a separate Managed Google Play store live inside the container; the rest of the phone — personal apps, photos, personal Google account — stays completely outside Intune's reach. Why it exists: Organizations need to protect corporate email and data on phones they don't own and can't dictate the full configuration of, while employees reasonably refuse to hand over total control of a device they paid for and use for their personal life. Work profile is Google and Microsoft's answer to that standoff.

Typical use cases
  • Executives and clinicians who want email/Teams on their personal phone
  • Field staff using their own Android device for light corporate access
  • Any BYOD policy where the org explicitly doesn't want device-wide control
Device ownership model

Personal property of the employee, end to end. Intune's device record marks ownership as "Personal." The org has zero claim to the hardware.

Prerequisites
  • Android 8.0+ device with Google Mobile Services (GMS)
  • Tenant bound to a managed Google Play account (Android Enterprise enabled in Intune)
  • Intune Company Portal app, installed by the user from the Play Store
Licensing requirements

Intune Plan 1 license assigned to the user (or an EMS/Microsoft 365 bundle that includes it). No device-side license needed since the device isn't org-owned.

Dependencies
  • Entra ID account for the user
  • Mobile Application Management / app protection policies if you want to layer MAM controls inside the work profile too
  • Enrollment restrictions in Intune must allow personally-owned Android devices
User experience during enrollment

User installs Company Portal from the regular Play Store, signs in with their work account, and is walked through creating the work profile. A distinct badge appears on work apps' icons on the home screen so personal and work apps are visually distinguishable.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Admin confirms Android Enterprise / managed Google Play binding exists in Intune.
  2. Admin creates and assigns a Work Profile enrollment configuration (or relies on the default).
  3. User installs Company Portal from Google Play on their own device.
  4. User signs in with work credentials; Company Portal detects eligibility and starts work profile setup.
  5. Android creates the encrypted work container; Managed Google Play apps begin installing automatically per assignment.
  6. User sets a separate work-profile security challenge if required by policy.
Management capabilities
  • Push/remove apps inside the work profile
  • Enforce passcode and encryption on the work side
  • Selective wipe of the work profile only
  • Block copy/paste from work to personal clipboard
  • Restrict screen capture of work apps
Security implications

Strong containment of corporate data, but the org has no visibility into the personal side — it cannot see if the phone's OS is out of date outside what device-wide compliance signals expose, cannot enforce personal-side passcodes, and cannot remote-wipe the entire device.

Limitations
  • No control over personal apps, personal OS settings, or non-work data
  • Cannot factory-reset the whole device remotely
  • Custom OMA-URI configuration profiles for this mode stopped being supported for new policies in April 2025 — use built-in settings catalog options instead
Advantages
  • High user acceptance — feels fair to BYOD users
  • Fast to deploy, no procurement involved
  • Strong data separation despite light footprint
Disadvantages
  • Weakest level of organizational control of any mode
  • No supervision-style restrictions available
  • User can simply uninstall the work profile, ending management instantly
Common mistakes admins make
  • Assuming they can wipe the whole personal phone in an offboarding scenario — they can only wipe the work profile
  • Forgetting to scope compliance policies separately for personal vs. corporate ownership
Best practices
  • Pair with an app protection (MAM) policy for defense in depth
  • Set clear enrollment restrictions so corporate-owned devices can't accidentally enroll as personal
  • Communicate up front exactly what IT can and can't see/do
ANDROID · MANAGEMENT MODE · AE-02

Work Profile — Corporate-Owned (COPE)

Current Corporate

What it is: The organization owns the device outright and has full device-owner control over it, but a work profile is still carved out inside it, so the employee can also install personal apps and use it as their daily phone. Google's current documentation calls this "work profile on a company-owned device"; almost everyone in the field still calls it COPE (Corporate-Owned, Personally Enabled). Why it exists: Some organizations want to issue a single phone per employee instead of two (one corporate, one personal) while still keeping full device-level control — remote wipe of the whole device, enforced device-wide settings — that personally-owned work profile can't offer.

Typical use cases
  • Field workers issued a single company phone they're allowed to also use personally
  • On-call clinical staff who need one device for both work paging apps and personal calls
  • Organizations replacing two-device policies with one
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned. Intune records ownership as "Corporate." The organization can factory-reset the entire device at will — this is the device's defining trade-off versus BYOD work profile.

Prerequisites
  • Android 11+ recommended (Google's currently supported COPE baseline; some OEMs require newer)
  • Device must be provisioned via a corporate-owned provisioning method (QR, token, zero-touch, or KME) — it cannot become COPE through the Play Store path used for BYOD
  • Managed Google Play binding in Intune
Licensing requirements

Intune Plan 1 (user- or device-based, depending on your tenant's licensing model) plus a Device Enrollment Manager account for bulk provisioning if used.

Dependencies
  • A provisioning method (most commonly a QR code or token) to kick off device-owner setup at first boot
  • Device naming template configuration (recommended) so bulk-enrolled phones are identifiable in the console
User experience during enrollment

The user (or IT, during pre-provisioning) scans a QR code or types a token at the Android setup wizard. The device sets itself up as device-owner, then automatically creates a work profile alongside it. The user can then sign into a personal Google account inside the personal side.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Admin creates a Corporate-Owned Work Profile enrollment profile in Intune and generates a QR code or token.
  2. Device is factory-reset (or new out of box).
  3. At the "Welcome" screen, the provisioning trigger is invoked (tap screen 6 times for QR, or type the token string).
  4. Device downloads the Intune app as the Device Policy Controller and sets device-owner mode.
  5. A work profile is automatically created inside the now-managed device.
  6. User signs in with work credentials to complete setup; personal Google sign-in is optional afterward.
Management capabilities
  • Everything available in personally-owned work profile, plus:
  • Full device wipe / factory reset remotely
  • Device-wide restrictions (Wi-Fi config, kiosk exceptions, lost-mode lock and locate)
  • Custom device naming templates
  • Bluetooth, USB, and personal-side restrictions the org can choose to apply
Security implications

Much stronger posture than BYOD work profile — the org can enforce device-wide encryption, a device-wide lock screen, and remote wipe of everything, including the personal side, on loss or offboarding.

Limitations
  • Requires the org to actually own and issue the hardware — real procurement and asset-management overhead
  • Cannot be converted in-place from an already-deployed personal or fully managed device; requires factory reset
  • A known issue reported across MDM community forums in 2025 affects COPE enrollment on some Samsung Android 16 devices — Knox Mobile Enrollment is the documented fallback while that's tracked
Advantages
  • One device per person instead of two
  • Full wipe capability protects the org if the device is lost or the employee leaves
  • Personal use still allowed, supporting adoption
Disadvantages
  • Procurement and lifecycle management burden falls on the org
  • Employees may feel uneasy that the org can wipe a device they also use personally
  • More setup complexity than either pure BYOD or pure fully managed
Common mistakes admins make
  • Provisioning via the wrong method and accidentally landing the device in Fully Managed instead of COPE (no personal profile created)
  • Not setting a device naming template before a bulk rollout, leaving hundreds of auto-named devices
Best practices
  • Pilot on one OEM/Android version combination before a bulk rollout, given known platform-specific enrollment bugs
  • Document an explicit "what IT can see and wipe" disclosure for employees, since this is a frequent source of pushback
ANDROID · MANAGEMENT MODE · AE-03

Fully Managed (Device Owner, no personal profile)

Current Corporate

What it is: The organization owns and controls the entire device, top to bottom, with no work-profile/personal split at all. Everything on the phone is visible and configurable by Intune. Why it exists: Some corporate devices are purely tools for the job — a delivery driver's scanner-phone, a single-purpose field tablet — where personal use was never intended and the org wants the simplest possible, fully transparent management model.

Typical use cases
  • Purpose-issued task phones with no personal-use expectation
  • Devices that will later be converted into a dedicated/kiosk role
  • High-security environments where any personal data on the device is an unacceptable risk
Device ownership model

Fully corporate-owned, recorded as "Corporate" in Intune. No legal or practical claim by the employee to any part of the device.

Prerequisites
  • Android 8.0+ device, GMS present
  • A corporate provisioning method (QR/token/zero-touch/KME)
  • Managed Google Play binding
Licensing requirements

Intune Plan 1 per device or per assigned user, same baseline as the other Android Enterprise modes.

Dependencies
  • Same provisioning trigger mechanics as COPE — the only difference is the enrollment profile type selected in Intune, which determines whether a work profile gets layered in afterward
User experience during enrollment

Identical first-boot flow to COPE provisioning (QR/token at setup wizard), but no personal-side Google sign-in step follows — the device stays fully managed end to end.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Admin creates a Fully Managed enrollment profile (as opposed to a Corporate-Owned Work Profile one) and generates the provisioning artifact.
  2. Device is factory reset and provisioned via QR, token, zero-touch, or KME, pointing at that profile.
  3. Device sets device-owner mode with no accompanying personal container.
  4. Compliance and configuration profiles assigned to the device's group apply immediately, device-wide.
Management capabilities
  • Complete device-wide configuration: Wi-Fi, VPN, certificates, app allow/block lists
  • Full remote wipe and factory reset
  • Lost mode, remote lock, location
  • Can be converted into a Dedicated (kiosk) device later via policy, without re-provisioning
Security implications

The strongest, simplest security posture of the four modes — there is no personal-data boundary to reason about, so policy can be maximally strict without any BYOD-style privacy trade-off.

Limitations
  • Zero personal use — a poor fit if employees expect to use the device outside work
  • Same factory-reset requirement to change modes later
Advantages
  • Simplest mode to reason about and audit
  • Maximum control with no privacy carve-outs to manage
  • Natural stepping-stone to Dedicated mode
Disadvantages
  • Employees often resent carrying two phones if personal use is expected elsewhere
  • Not appropriate for BYOD under any circumstance
Common mistakes admins make
  • Choosing Fully Managed when COPE was actually what the business wanted, then fielding complaints that staff "can't even add their personal account"
Best practices
  • Confirm the personal-use expectation with the business owner before picking this mode over COPE — it's the single most consequential decision in the whole Android rollout
ANDROID · MANAGEMENT MODE · AE-04

Dedicated Device (Kiosk, formerly COSU)

Current Corporate

What it is: A Fully Managed device locked to one app, or a tightly curated small set of apps, with the rest of the OS hidden from the user — no home screen, no settings, no way to back out. Why it exists: Many corporate Android devices aren't general-purpose phones at all — they're a check-in screen, a barcode scanner, a digital sign, or a single-purpose clinical app on a wall-mounted tablet. Dedicated mode (Google's old term was "corporate-owned, single-use," now deprecated) is purpose-built for exactly that.

Typical use cases
  • Reception check-in kiosks
  • Barcode/asset scanners
  • Digital signage
  • Single-purpose clinical intake tablets
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned, "Corporate" in Intune — there is no scenario where Dedicated mode is appropriate for a personal device.

Prerequisites
  • Android 8.0+, ideally a dedicated-hardware-friendly OEM build
  • A Fully Managed enrollment profile with kiosk/Lock Task settings applied on top
  • The app(s) to be locked down already published to the Managed Google Play catalog
Licensing requirements

Same Intune Plan 1 baseline; per-device licensing is the common model here since there's frequently no individually assigned user.

Dependencies
  • A Device Enrollment Manager account is strongly recommended for bulk provisioning many kiosk units
  • Kiosk configuration profile (single-app or multi-app Lock Task mode)
User experience during enrollment

Typically zero end-user involvement — IT or a DEM account provisions the device before it ever reaches the front desk or wall mount. The "user" of the finished kiosk never sees a setup wizard at all.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Admin creates a Fully Managed enrollment profile and a kiosk configuration profile specifying allowed app(s).
  2. Device provisioned via QR/token/zero-touch/KME against that profile.
  3. Kiosk configuration profile applies, locking the launcher to the designated app(s).
  4. Device is deployed to its physical location (reception desk, wall mount, scanner dock).
Management capabilities
  • Single- or multi-app Lock Task mode
  • Disable status bar, home/recents buttons, notifications
  • Auto-launch app on boot/crash recovery
  • Branded boot screens and wallpaper
Security implications

Very low attack surface by design — there's nothing for an opportunistic user to get into beyond the one locked app, which also limits the blast radius if the device is tampered with physically.

Limitations
  • Poor fit for anything requiring flexible, multi-app general use
  • Some OEM-specific quirks around exiting Lock Task mode for maintenance
Advantages
  • Tamper-resistant by design
  • Predictable, supportable, easy to remotely troubleshoot since the app set is tiny
Disadvantages
  • Inflexible if business requirements change app needs frequently
  • Maintenance (OS updates, app updates) needs a deliberate remote process since there's no user to manually intervene
Common mistakes admins make
  • Forgetting to allow a maintenance escape mechanism, leaving no way to debug a stuck device on-site without a factory reset
Best practices
  • Build in a hidden admin gesture or PIN-protected exit for field troubleshooting
  • Use a DEM account and device naming templates for any fleet beyond a handful of units
Fig. 3 — Android management-mode decision tree. Ownership and personal-use intent are the only two questions that matter; everything else follows from them.

Provisioning methods (the "how")

These six mechanisms are how a device actually arrives at one of the four modes above. Several can usually be substituted for each other depending on how the hardware was sourced and how hands-on IT wants to be.

ANDROID · PROVISIONING METHOD · AE-P1

Zero-Touch Enrollment

CurrentCorporate

What it is: Google's reseller-integrated provisioning system. A device bought through a certified zero-touch reseller is pre-registered to your organization before it ships; the very first time it's powered on and connects to a network, it automatically pulls down your enrollment configuration with no manual trigger at all. Why it exists: Large corporate-owned rollouts (hundreds or thousands of units) can't scale if every device needs someone to scan a QR code by hand.

Typical use cases
  • Large fleet rollouts (hundreds+ devices) of Fully Managed or COPE phones
  • Multi-OEM corporate deployments where you don't want to rely on Samsung-only tooling
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned only — zero-touch has no BYOD use case, since it depends on a controlled purchase channel.

Prerequisites
  • Devices purchased through a certified zero-touch reseller or carrier (not retail)
  • Android 9.0+ device support for zero-touch
  • A configured zero-touch customer account, linked to your EMM/Intune
Licensing requirements

No extra Google fee; standard Intune Plan 1 still applies per device/user.

Dependencies
  • Reseller must actually register devices into your zero-touch portal account at time of sale — a step IT often has to chase the vendor on
User experience

The closest thing to magic in this whole guide: power on, connect to Wi-Fi, and the device configures itself. No codes, no taps, no IT interaction required at the desk.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Devices purchased through a zero-touch-enabled reseller, tied to your organization's zero-touch account.
  2. Admin creates a zero-touch configuration in the Google zero-touch portal, pointing at Intune as the EMM and selecting Fully Managed or COPE.
  3. Configuration assigned to the batch of devices (by reseller order, IMEI, or default).
  4. End user or IT powers the device on for the first time; it self-provisions against that configuration.
Management capabilities

Identical to whichever mode (Fully Managed or COPE) it's configured to provision into — zero-touch itself adds no capabilities, it's purely a delivery mechanism.

Security implications

Strong: the device is bound to your org's identity before a user ever touches it, closing the window where an unmanaged device could exist.

Limitations
  • Reseller dependency — doesn't work for devices bought retail or already in your hands
  • Doesn't directly support Dedicated mode provisioning; you provision Fully Managed via zero-touch, then layer kiosk configuration afterward
Advantages
  • True zero-touch at scale
  • Works across OEMs, not Samsung-specific
Disadvantages
  • Procurement-channel dependent; retrofitting devices you already own isn't possible this way
Common mistakes admins make
  • Assuming any reseller supports zero-touch — confirm certification before procurement, not after
Best practices
  • Bake zero-touch reseller requirements into your procurement policy up front
  • Set a default configuration in the zero-touch portal so devices never sit unassigned
ANDROID · PROVISIONING METHOD · AE-P2

Samsung Knox Mobile Enrollment (KME)

CurrentCorporate

What it is: Samsung's own equivalent to zero-touch, exclusively for Samsung Galaxy devices, run through the Knox Admin Portal. Why it exists: Samsung supports zero-touch too, but KME predates it and adds Samsung-specific extras (Knox attestation, on-premise EMM support, Dual DAR encryption) that pure Android zero-touch doesn't offer — which is exactly why a Samsung-heavy environment like HNZ's Knox/Android Enterprise estate leans on it.

Typical use cases
  • Samsung-only or Samsung-majority fleets
  • Environments needing on-premise EMM enrollment via KME Direct
  • Anywhere COPE provisioning is currently misbehaving on stock zero-touch for a given Samsung/Android 16 build, per the 2025 community-reported issue noted earlier
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned. Devices must be uploaded into the Knox Admin Portal under your organization's Knox account.

Prerequisites
  • Samsung Knox-compatible device (Knox 2.4+)
  • An approved Samsung Knox account (approval takes 1–2 business days)
  • Devices added to the Knox Admin Portal, by reseller integration or manual IMEI upload
Licensing requirements

Knox Mobile Enrollment itself is free with a Knox account; standard Intune licensing still applies per device.

Dependencies
  • Knox Admin Portal profile configured to point at Intune
  • Note: KME's old "Device Admin (Deprecated)" profile type should never be used for new enrollments — always select the Android Enterprise profile type in Knox
User experience

Same hands-off experience as zero-touch: power on, connect, auto-provision. KME Direct (a PC application) also lets IT pre-stage devices over USB in bulk before they ever reach end users, even without internet at the point of staging.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Register for a Knox account; wait for Samsung approval.
  2. Add devices to the Knox Admin Portal (via reseller integration or IMEI/serial upload).
  3. Create an Android Enterprise profile in Knox pointing at Intune, choosing Fully Managed or Work Profile (COPE).
  4. Assign the profile to the device batch.
  5. Device self-provisions on first boot, or via KME Direct pre-staging.
Management capabilities

Whatever the target mode provides, plus Samsung Knox extras like Dual Data-at-Rest encryption and Knox-attested device health checks available to the EMM.

Security implications

Adds a hardware root-of-trust attestation layer specific to Samsung Knox on top of standard Android Enterprise security.

Limitations
  • Samsung devices only
  • Knox account approval adds lead time to a rollout plan
  • The legacy Device Admin (Deprecated) KME profile type no longer works on Android 11+ — a frequent trap when following outdated tutorials
Advantages
  • Mature, Samsung-optimized, supports on-premise/offline staging via KME Direct
  • Documented fallback when stock COPE zero-touch misbehaves on certain Samsung builds
Disadvantages
  • Vendor lock to Samsung hardware
  • Account approval lead time
Common mistakes admins make
  • Selecting the deprecated Device Admin profile type out of habit from old documentation
  • Not budgeting 1–2 business days for Knox account approval before a deployment deadline
Best practices
  • Always choose the Android Enterprise (not Device Admin) profile type in the Knox Admin Portal
  • Use KME Direct for pre-staging in low-connectivity sites
ANDROID · PROVISIONING METHOD · AE-P3

QR Code Enrollment

CurrentCorporate

What it is: Intune generates a QR code encoding the provisioning details (server, Wi-Fi, profile type); the device's setup wizard scans it (after a six-tap gesture on the welcome screen) and self-configures. Why it exists: A reseller- and Samsung-account-free way to manually provision corporate devices without typing long strings — practical for small to mid-size batches, retail-bought hardware, or one-off devices.

Typical use cases
  • Small/medium corporate device batches
  • Retail-purchased devices ineligible for zero-touch/KME
  • IT staging devices by hand before distribution
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned (Fully Managed or COPE, selected when generating the profile).

Prerequisites
  • Android 8.0+, device factory-reset or out of box
  • Camera on the device being provisioned, or a second device to display the code
  • Wi-Fi network reachable at setup if encoded into the QR payload
Licensing requirements

No additional cost; standard Intune Plan 1 licensing.

Dependencies
  • Intune enrollment profile of the desired mode, with "generate QR code" selected
User experience

At the welcome screen, tap the screen six times in the same spot to trigger QR scanning mode, connect to Wi-Fi if prompted, then scan the printed or displayed code.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Admin creates an enrollment profile (Fully Managed, COPE, or Dedicated) in Intune.
  2. Admin downloads/generates the QR code for that profile.
  3. Device factory reset to out-of-box state.
  4. At welcome screen, six-tap gesture starts QR provisioning; device camera scans the code.
  5. Device downloads the DPC and completes setup against the encoded configuration.
Management capabilities

Whatever the chosen target mode supports — QR is purely a delivery mechanism, same as zero-touch and KME.

Security implications

Relies on physical/manual handling, so there's a window where the device sits unmanaged until someone actually scans the code — a smaller-scale operational risk than the other methods.

Limitations
  • Manual, so it doesn't scale gracefully to very large fleets
  • Internet connectivity is required by default for provisioning on Android 13+
Advantages
  • No reseller relationship or vendor account needed
  • Works on virtually any Android Enterprise-eligible device
Disadvantages
  • Manual, technician-time-intensive at scale
Common mistakes admins make
  • Forgetting the six-tap gesture only works on a truly factory-reset device — it silently fails on a device that's already been through Setup Wizard once
Best practices
  • Use for pilots and small batches; graduate to zero-touch/KME once volume justifies it

DPC Identifier / Token Enrollment (afw#setup)

CurrentCorporate

What it is: Instead of scanning a code, the user manually types afw#setup (Android for Work setup) into the Google account field at the very start of the out-of-box wizard. This downloads the Intune app as the Device Policy Controller and proceeds with corporate provisioning. Why it exists: A no-camera, no-network-config-needed fallback that works on essentially any Android Enterprise device with nothing more than a keyboard.

Typical use cases
  • Devices without a usable camera at setup time
  • Remote/phone-guided IT support walking a user through setup verbally
  • Simple single-device provisioning
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned, same target modes (Fully Managed or COPE) as QR.

Prerequisites
  • Factory-reset Android 6.0+ device
  • Network connectivity to download the DPC after the token is entered
Licensing requirements

No extra cost; standard Intune Plan 1.

Dependencies
  • An enrollment profile already created in Intune of the desired target mode
User experience

At the "Sign in" screen during setup, the user types afw#setup instead of an email address, then follows prompts to sign in with work credentials and complete provisioning.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Admin ensures the desired enrollment profile exists in Intune.
  2. Device factory reset to out-of-box state.
  3. At the account sign-in step, user types afw#setup.
  4. Device downloads Intune as DPC, then prompts for work account sign-in to pick up the matching profile.
Management capabilities

Identical to the resulting mode (Fully Managed/COPE) — again, purely a delivery mechanism.

Security implications

Same unmanaged-until-provisioned window as QR; relies on someone correctly typing the token, which introduces light human error risk (typos).

Limitations
  • Entirely manual, one device at a time
  • No QR/camera shortcut, so slightly slower per device for sighted users
Advantages
  • Works without a camera or printed code
  • Easy to talk someone through over the phone
Disadvantages
  • Doesn't scale past small batches
Common mistakes admins make
  • Typing the token into the wrong field (it must go in the Google account email field specifically, not a generic search box)

  • Keep this method in your back pocket for remote-support and edge-case scenarios rather than as a primary rollout method
ANDROID · PROVISIONING METHOD · AE-P5

NFC Tag Enrollment

Current (narrow use)Corporate

What it is: A physical NFC tag is pre-programmed with provisioning data; tapping a factory-reset device against the tag during setup triggers provisioning. Why it exists: Extremely fast physical bulk-staging when IT is physically handling a stack of devices — tap, tap, tap, no typing or scanning required. Important distinction: this is different from the old "NFC Beam" (phone-to-phone bump) feature, which Google deprecated starting with Android 10 in favor of this tag-based approach.

Typical use cases
  • Physical staging stations where IT processes many devices by hand
  • Environments without reliable Wi-Fi/QR options during staging
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned, same target modes as QR/token.

Prerequisites
  • NFC-capable Android device, NFC-capable provisioner device, and a programmable NFC tag
  • Recommended for Android 10+ specifically because NFC Beam is deprecated
Licensing requirements

No extra cost; standard Intune Plan 1.

Dependencies
  • A separate NFC programmer app/device to write the provisioning payload onto the physical tag
User experience

IT taps the target device against the programmed tag at the welcome screen; provisioning starts automatically.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Admin creates the enrollment profile in Intune.
  2. Provisioning payload written to a physical NFC tag using a programmer app.
  3. Factory-reset device tapped against the tag at the welcome screen.
  4. Device downloads DPC and completes provisioning against the encoded profile.
Management capabilities

Identical to whichever target mode is encoded — again, a delivery mechanism only.

Security implications

Physical proximity requirement (tap distance) limits opportunistic misuse versus a broadcast code.

Limitations
  • Niche today — most organizations have moved to QR or token for manual staging since they need no extra hardware
  • NFC Beam (the old peer-to-peer variant) doesn't work at all from Android 10 onward
Advantages
  • Very fast physical, one-handed staging once tags are programmed
Disadvantages
  • Requires extra hardware (tags, programmer) most orgs don't already own
Common mistakes admins make
  • Confusing this with the deprecated NFC Beam/Bump and assuming it no longer works at all
Best practices
  • Only invest in NFC tag tooling if you're doing high-volume physical staging; otherwise QR/token cover the same ground with less hardware
ANDROID · PROVISIONING METHOD · AE-P6

Company Portal App Enrollment (BYOD Work Profile)

CurrentBYOD

What it is: The provisioning method behind Work Profile (BYOD), covered fully under AE-01 above — included here as its own entry because, unlike the other five methods, it's the only path into a personally-owned work profile, and it deserves to be named explicitly as a "method" rather than buried inside the mode description.

Typical use cases

Any BYOD work profile rollout — see AE-01 for the full picture.

Device ownership model

Personal, always.

Workflow in one line

User installs Company Portal from Google Play (never sideloaded, never from an MDM-pushed source, since the device isn't managed yet) → signs in → work profile auto-creates.

Best practice

Make sure your enrollment restrictions explicitly permit personally-owned Android devices, or users will hit a blocked-enrollment error with no clear explanation.

Legacy & deprecated (Android)

Deprecated — do not use for new enrollments

Android Device Administrator (DA) management. The original, much weaker Android management API that predates Android Enterprise entirely. Google deprecated it in 2020; Microsoft ended Intune support for device administrator management on any device with access to Google Mobile Services (GMS) at the end of 2024, and by mid-2026 it's fully retired for GMS devices. It survives only in a narrow corner case: devices that genuinely lack GMS (some specialty or region-locked hardware), where Intune still documents a device-administrator path because no Android Enterprise alternative exists for them. If you inherit an environment still running DA-managed GMS devices, migrating them off device administrator and onto a current Android Enterprise mode should be treated as a priority, not a someday task.

Deprecated — superseded

NFC Beam / NFC Bump (peer-to-peer NFC provisioning, distinct from the tag-based NFC enrollment above). Deprecated starting with Android 10. If you find tutorials describing "bump two phones together to enroll," they're describing this retired mechanism — the tag-based method (AE-P5) is its replacement.

Terminology retired, concept current

"Corporate-Owned Single-Use" (COSU). Not gone — just renamed. Google's current term is Dedicated device (AE-04). If you see COSU in older blog posts, vendor docs, or exam material, mentally substitute "Dedicated device."

Custom profile path retired for one mode

Custom OMA-URI configuration profiles for personally-owned work profile devices. Microsoft ended support for creating new custom profiles of this type in April 2025. Existing ones still apply but won't be supported going forward; use the built-in settings catalog and dedicated policy types instead. This restriction is specific to personally-owned work profile devices — custom profiles for device administrator devices are a separate, unaffected case (itself a legacy path, as noted above).

§2iOS / iPadOS device enrollment

Apple's model splits along a cleaner line than Android's: ownership decides almost everything up front. Organization-owned devices bought through the right channel get Automated Device Enrollment (ADE) and full supervision; personally-owned devices get a lighter-touch User Enrollment. There's no real equivalent to Android's four-mode spectrum — instead there's a sharper fork, with a couple of bridging options for devices that fall outside the clean cases.

Naming note, read this once

Throughout this section you'll see Apple Business used as the current name for what was, until April 14, 2026, called Apple Business Manager (ABM). The mechanics — tokens, ADE, Managed Apple Accounts — are unchanged. Microsoft's own Intune documentation switched to "Apple Business" in its April–May 2026 updates, but you'll still hear "ABM" everywhere in practice, including from Apple's own legacy URLs. This guide uses "Apple Business" going forward and calls out "ABM" parenthetically where it helps.

iOS/iPadOS · ENROLLMENT METHOD · IOS-01

Automated Device Enrollment (ADE) — Setup Assistant with Modern Authentication

Current · recommendedCorporate

What it is: Apple's zero-touch enrollment for organization-owned iPhones and iPads, using Apple's modern, MFA-capable authentication during the on-device Setup Assistant. The device is linked to your organization in Apple Business before it ships; first power-on finds Intune automatically. Why it exists: Hospitals, retailers, schools — anyone deploying corporate-owned Apple hardware at any real scale — needs devices to arrive pre-configured and supervised without a technician manually touching each one.

Typical use cases
  • Clinician-issued iPhones
  • Executive corporate-owned iPhones/iPads
  • Any organization-owned Apple device needing supervision-level restrictions
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned, supervised. This is the only iOS method in this guide that yields a supervised device.

Prerequisites
  • New or wiped iOS/iPadOS device purchased through an eligible channel that feeds Apple Business
  • Intune Plan 1 (minimum) for iOS/iPadOS management
  • An Apple MDM push certificate configured in Intune
  • An active ADE token (.p7m file) linking Apple Business to Intune
Licensing requirements

Intune Plan 1 per assigned user/device; Apple Business itself is free to register for.

Dependencies
  • Apple Business account, approved and configured
  • Devices synced from Apple Business to Intune and assigned to an enrollment policy
  • Company Portal deployed through Intune itself (never the App Store version) for automatic delivery and updates
User experience during enrollment

User unboxes the device, connects to Wi-Fi, and Setup Assistant walks them through signing in with Entra ID credentials (with MFA if required) — Company Portal is pre-delivered, so there's no separate app-store detour. Enrollment SSO can carry that one authentication through to subsequent app sign-ins.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Get an Apple MDM push certificate and add it to Intune.
  2. Create an MDM server token for Intune inside Apple Business; download the .p7m token.
  3. Upload the token to Intune, creating the trust relationship.
  4. New/wiped devices purchased through Apple Business automatically sync into Intune's device list.
  5. Create an ADE enrollment profile choosing "Setup Assistant with modern authentication," and assign it to the synced devices.
  6. Deploy Company Portal through Intune as a required app for the assignment group.
  7. Device is wiped/reset, then distributed; on first boot it pulls down the policy automatically.
Management capabilities
  • Full supervised-device restriction set (block AirDrop, silently install/remove apps, Single App Mode, content filtering)
  • Declarative Device Management (DDM) by default for fast, proactive compliance reporting
  • Platform SSO support and Enrollment SSO for streamlined auth
  • Device-wide compliance and configuration profiles
Security implications

The strongest posture available on iOS — supervision plus mandatory, tamper-resistant enrollment (the user cannot remove the management profile) makes this the right baseline for any device handling protected health information.

Limitations
  • Requires devices to come from an eligible purchase channel — a device bought at a generic consumer retailer typically doesn't enter ADE
  • Wiping is required before re-enrollment if a device was previously set up differently
Advantages
  • True zero-touch at scale, same promise as Android zero-touch
  • Maximum management depth of any iOS method
  • Enforced, non-removable enrollment
Disadvantages
  • Purchase-channel dependency
  • Full reset needed to bring an already-deployed device into ADE after the fact
Common mistakes admins make
  • Pushing Company Portal via the App Store instead of through Intune — this breaks automatic updates and isn't supported for ADE devices
  • Forgetting to renew the ADE and MDM push certificate tokens before they expire, silently breaking enrollment for everyone
Best practices
  • Calendar-remind certificate/token renewals well ahead of expiry
  • Default to "Setup Assistant with modern authentication" over the legacy option for every new ADE profile
iOS/iPadOS · ENROLLMENT METHOD · IOS-02

ADE via Apple Configurator (Manual Assignment)

CurrentCorporate

What it is: A way to manually add devices into ADE that didn't arrive through an automatically-syncing purchase channel — for example, devices bought before your Apple Business account existed, or transferred from another part of the business. Apple Configurator (a Mac app) reads each device's serial number and assigns it into Apple Business directly. Why it exists: Not every corporate-owned Apple device follows a clean reseller-to-Apple-Business pipeline; this is the manual on-ramp for the rest.

Typical use cases
  • Pre-existing corporate devices bought before Apple Business was set up
  • Devices transferred between business units or acquired through a merger
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned, supervised — once assigned, it follows the same ADE workflow as IOS-01.

Prerequisites
  • A Mac running Apple Configurator
  • Physical access to each device (USB connection) for the initial assignment step
  • An Apple Business account with appropriate role permissions
Licensing requirements

Same Intune Plan 1 baseline as standard ADE; Apple Configurator itself is free.

Dependencies
  • Device must be erased/reset before reassignment if it was previously configured under a personal Apple ID
User experience

Once assigned, identical to standard ADE — Setup Assistant with modern authentication picks up from there.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Connect device via USB to a Mac running Apple Configurator.
  2. Erase the device if it has an existing personal configuration.
  3. Use Configurator to assign the device's serial number into your Apple Business account.
  4. Device now appears in Apple Business/Intune sync, and follows the standard ADE profile assignment flow from there.
Management capabilities

Identical to standard ADE post-assignment.

Security implications

Same end state as ADE, but with a manual assignment step that introduces a brief unmanaged window per device.

Limitations
  • Requires hands-on, one-device-at-a-time USB connection — doesn't scale like a reseller-fed pipeline
  • Needs a Mac, which not every Windows-centric IT shop has readily available
Advantages
  • Brings "orphaned" devices into the same supervised, zero-touch-on-reset state as properly channel-purchased ones
Disadvantages
  • Manual, slower, Mac-dependent
Common mistakes admins make
  • Forgetting to erase a device that already has a personal Apple ID signed in before reassigning it, leading to a confusing partial-state device
Best practices
  • Use this once to clean up legacy inventory, then route every future purchase through a properly linked Apple Business reseller relationship so this step isn't needed again
iOS/iPadOS · ENROLLMENT METHOD · IOS-03

Device Enrollment with Company Portal (Direct Enrollment)

Current, narrower role todayBYOD-leaning

What it is: A full-device MDM enrollment performed by the user themselves through the Company Portal app — the device becomes fully managed (not just a User Enrollment-style partial management), but without ADE's supervision or zero-touch delivery. Why it exists: Covers personal or corporate-owned devices that need full MDM management but aren't going through ADE — for example, a personal iPhone where the organization needs more control than User Enrollment allows, or a corporate device acquired outside ADE's reach that still needs full management rather than just User Enrollment's lighter touch.

Typical use cases
  • Personal devices where the organization needs full device management, not just app-level control
  • Corporate devices that can't go through ADE for some reason but still need full MDM depth
Device ownership model

Configurable — can be marked personal or corporate depending on the enrollment policy and organizational process, but the device is always unsupervised since it didn't go through ADE.

Prerequisites
  • Company Portal installed from the App Store (the standard public path, unlike ADE devices)
  • Apple MDM push certificate configured in Intune
  • iOS 13+ recommended
Licensing requirements

Intune Plan 1 per user.

Dependencies
  • An enrollment policy in Intune configured for "Device enrollment with Company Portal" rather than account-driven user enrollment
User experience

User installs Company Portal from the App Store, signs in, and is walked through installing a management profile from Settings — a more hands-on, multi-step process than ADE or account-driven enrollment.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Admin creates an enrollment policy choosing "Device enrollment with Company Portal."
  2. User installs Company Portal app from the App Store.
  3. User signs in with work account.
  4. Company Portal directs the user to Settings to download and install the management profile.
  5. User confirms installation; device becomes fully managed.
Management capabilities

Full device-level MDM management (similar depth to ADE's configuration/compliance capability), but without supervision-only restrictions — the user retains the ability to remove the management profile, ending enrollment.

Security implications

Stronger than User Enrollment but weaker than ADE — the org can see and configure more of the device, but enforcement isn't tamper-resistant the way ADE's mandatory profile is.

Limitations
  • Not supervised — several restriction categories simply aren't available
  • User can opt out at any time by removing the management profile
Advantages
  • Deeper management than User Enrollment without requiring ADE/procurement-channel purchase
Disadvantages
  • On a personal device, full MDM visibility raises real privacy concerns for end users — most organizations now prefer Account-Driven User Enrollment for BYOD specifically to avoid this
Common mistakes admins make
  • Defaulting every BYOD iPhone to this method out of habit, when Account-Driven User Enrollment is the better-scoped, more privacy-respecting current choice
Best practices
  • Reserve this for corporate-owned edge cases that can't go through ADE; use Account-Driven User Enrollment for genuine BYOD
iOS/iPadOS · ENROLLMENT METHOD · IOS-04

Account-Driven User Enrollment

Current · recommended for BYODBYOD

What it is: Apple's current, privacy-respecting BYOD enrollment. The user adds their work account directly through Settings → VPN & Device Management — no Company Portal app required to initiate it — and a separate, cryptographically isolated managed Apple Account partition is created on the device specifically for work data. Why it exists: Replaces the older profile-based User Enrollment with a cleaner architecture and stronger personal/work data separation, and is Apple's clear current direction for personally-owned device management.

Typical use cases
  • Any personal iPhone/iPad accessing corporate email, Teams, or other Microsoft 365 data
  • Executives and clinicians who decline a corporate-issued device
Device ownership model

Personal, unsupervised, by design — Apple deliberately withholds supervision-grade controls from any User Enrollment variant to protect personal device owners.

Prerequisites
  • iOS/iPadOS with account-driven enrollment support (recent iOS versions)
  • Apple MDM push certificate configured in Intune
  • An enrollment policy in Intune set to account-driven user enrollment
Licensing requirements

Intune Plan 1 per user.

Dependencies
  • Enrollment SSO support helps reduce repeated authentication prompts during setup
User experience

User goes to Settings → VPN & Device Management → Sign in to work or school account, authenticates, and the managed Apple Account partition is created automatically. The Company Portal web app can be pinned to the home screen afterward for status checks, but isn't required to start enrollment.

Enrollment workflow
  1. Admin configures an enrollment policy for account-driven user enrollment.
  2. User opens Settings → General → VPN & Device Management and adds their work account.
  3. Device authenticates the user via Entra ID, with MFA if configured.
  4. iOS creates a separate managed Apple Account partition for work data, leaving the rest of the device untouched.
  5. Optionally, the admin deploys the Company Portal web app shortcut for ongoing device status visibility.
Management capabilities
  • Manage and selectively wipe only the work-data partition
  • Push managed apps and configuration scoped to work data
  • Enforce a separate passcode for the work partition if required
Security implications

Strong cryptographic separation between personal and work data, with zero visibility into the personal side — the closest iOS equivalent to Android's work profile philosophy.

Limitations
  • No supervision-level restrictions, ever
  • Smaller management surface than full MDM enrollment by design
Advantages
  • Best-in-class privacy story for BYOD on iOS today
  • No app-store detour required to start enrollment
  • Apple's clear current direction — investing here pays off long-term
Disadvantages
  • Limited management depth versus a corporate device — appropriate trade-off for BYOD, but not a substitute when full control is genuinely required
Common mistakes admins make
  • Still pointing BYOD users at the deprecated profile-based Company Portal User Enrollment out of old habit or outdated internal documentation
Best practices
  • Make this your default BYOD iOS enrollment policy going forward
  • Pair with an app protection (MAM) policy on Outlook/Teams for layered data protection
iOS/iPadOS · ENROLLMENT METHOD · IOS-05

Shared iPad

CurrentCorporate

What it is: A single corporate-owned iPad, enrolled via ADE without user affinity, configured so multiple people can sign in and out, each getting their own private partition for apps, data, and preferences. Why it exists: Ward iPads, shift-based clinical tablets, and shared front-desk devices need to serve many different staff members across a day without mixing anyone's data — buying one iPad per nurse per shift isn't realistic.

Typical use cases
  • Nursing-station or ward iPads shared across shifts
  • Any rotating-staff scenario needing personalized but temporary access
Device ownership model

Corporate-owned, supervised, enrolled without user affinity (the device isn't tied to one person at enrollment time).

Prerequisites
  • iPad meeting Shared iPad's minimum hardware/storage requirements
  • ADE enrollment via Apple Business/School Manager
  • Entra ID federated with Apple Business so sign-in can use Entra credentials directly
Licensing requirements

Intune Plan 1 per device; no extra per-user fee for the shared model itself.

Dependencies
  • A dynamic device group to auto-assign the Shared iPad enrollment policy
  • Federation between Entra ID and Apple Business for Managed Apple Account auto-creation
User experience

A nurse picks up any Shared iPad on the ward, signs in with their Entra ID username and password, and lands in their own personalized partition — apps, recent documents, and preferences exactly as they left them, even if it's a physically different iPad than last time (with iCloud backup of partitions enabled).

Enrollment workflow
  1. Go to Devices > iOS/iPadOS > Enrollment > Enrollment program tokens; choose the token, then Profiles.
  2. Create an iOS/iPadOS profile; under management settings, set User affinity to "Enroll without user affinity," Supervised to Yes, and Shared iPad to Yes.
  3. Assign the profile to the target devices, ideally via a dynamic group.
  4. Federate Entra ID with Apple Business so a Managed Apple Account is auto-created matching each user's Entra username on first sign-in.
  5. Distribute iPads to the ward/unit; staff sign in and out as needed.
Management capabilities
  • Per-user partitioning with optional iCloud backup of partitions for continuity across devices
  • All standard supervised-device restrictions, applied device-wide
  • Configurable maximum number of cached user partitions per device
Security implications

Strong per-user data isolation on shared hardware — exactly the property a multi-staff clinical environment needs to stay compliant with patient-data handling expectations.

Limitations
  • Several Conditional Access configurations aren't supported with Shared iPad — specifically conditions requiring an approved client app, an app protection policy, per-device terms of use, device-compliance marking, or device filters
  • User-assigned policies don't show up cleanly in Intune device-based reports
Advantages
  • Purpose-built for exactly the shared-clinical-device problem
  • Entra ID federation makes sign-in frictionless and familiar to staff
Disadvantages
  • Conditional Access gaps mean you can't lean on every CA control you might use elsewhere
  • Minimum hardware requirements rule out older/cheaper iPad models
Common mistakes admins make
  • Assuming all Conditional Access policies will work unchanged on Shared iPad and discovering the unsupported list only after rollout
Best practices
  • Review the Shared iPad Conditional Access limitations before designing your access policies, not after
  • Federate Entra ID with Apple Business early — it's what makes the sign-in experience feel native to staff
Apple School Manager (ASM) — mentioned for completeness

ASM is Apple Business's education-sector sibling, used by schools and universities rather than businesses or health systems. It uses identical ADE mechanics to everything described above — the only practical difference for an Intune admin is which Apple portal issued the token. For a healthcare tenant like HNZ, Apple Business is the relevant one; ASM is included here purely so the picture is complete, per the original ask.

Legacy & deprecated (iOS / iPadOS)

Deprecated — no longer available for new enrollments

Apple User Enrollment with Company Portal (profile-based). The predecessor to Account-Driven User Enrollment. As of Intune's April 2026 documentation, this enrollment option is deprecated and unavailable for newly enrolled devices — existing devices already enrolled this way continue to receive support, but every new BYOD rollout should use Account-Driven User Enrollment (IOS-04) instead.

Legacy — not recommended

Setup Assistant (Legacy) for ADE. Still selectable as an authentication option when creating an ADE enrollment policy, but Microsoft no longer recommends it — it lacks the multifactor authentication and just-in-time registration support that "Setup Assistant with modern authentication" provides. Choose modern authentication for every new ADE profile.

Ending — legacy software-update commands

Legacy MDM software-update commands and payloads. With iOS 26, iPadOS 26, and macOS 26, Apple deprecated the older software-update command set in favor of Declarative Device Management's update model, and Intune is winding down support for the legacy commands accordingly. Not an enrollment method itself, but worth knowing if you're auditing older update-policy configurations alongside an enrollment refresh.

§5Real-world scenario: Contoso Healthcare

One fictional health system, used consistently. Contoso Healthcare runs a mixed Apple/Android estate across hospitals, clinics, and admin offices — structurally similar to the kind of environment a Workspace Administrator at a real health system manages day to day. Here's how each role actually gets a device, and why that specific method was chosen over the alternatives.

Clinical · Doctors

Corporate iPhones

Doctors are issued a corporate iPhone for secure messaging, e-prescribing apps, and Teams. Contoso needs full supervision — silently pushing clinical apps, blocking AirDrop of patient photos, enforcing managed-app-only data flow — none of which a BYOD method could deliver.

ADE — Setup Assistant, modern auth (IOS-01)
Clinical · Nurses

Shared ward iPads

Each ward has a pool of iPads used across a shift by whichever nurse is on duty. Buying one iPad per nurse isn't realistic, and patient data absolutely cannot bleed between users sharing the same physical device.

Shared iPad (IOS-05)
Operations · Field workers

Community health Android phones

Community nurses visiting patients at home are issued one Android phone they're explicitly allowed to also use personally, rather than carrying two devices. Contoso still needs the ability to fully wipe the device if it's lost in the field.

Work Profile — COPE (AE-02), via Knox Mobile Enrollment
Facilities · Reception

Check-in kiosks

Front-desk Android tablets run a single patient check-in app, nothing else, all day. There's no individual user, no personal use, and the device needs to survive being touched by hundreds of strangers a day without ever exposing a home screen.

Dedicated device (AE-04), via QR provisioning
Leadership · Executives

BYOD smartphones

Executives decline corporate-issued hardware and want email and Teams on their own iPhone or Android phone instead, with minimal IT intrusion into a device they also use for their personal life.

Account-Driven User Enrollment (IOS-04) or Work Profile — BYOD (AE-01)
Telehealth · Consultation rooms

Wall-mounted consultation tablets

A dedicated telehealth room runs a supervised iPad locked to the Teams meeting app for remote consultations, joined anonymously without any individual clinician sign-in needed for the device itself.

ADE supervised + Single App Mode (built on IOS-01)

§6Administrator perspective

Stepping back from individual methods — here's what actually shows up in the Intune admin center, organized by the task categories an admin works through, not by platform.

Enrollment profiles

Android

Under Devices > Android, you create separate enrollment profile types per mode: Corporate-owned fully managed, Corporate-owned work profile (COPE), Corporate-owned dedicated, and personally-owned work profile is mostly governed by enrollment restrictions rather than a profile you build by hand. Each corporate profile generates the QR code or token artifact admins hand off to provisioning.

iOS / iPadOS

Under Devices > Enrollment > Apple, ADE profiles are created per Apple Business token and assigned to synced devices or device groups. User Enrollment is configured as an enrollment type (account-driven vs. legacy Company Portal) rather than a device-specific profile.

Tokens and certificates

Android

No certificate to manage directly — the trust relationship is the managed Google Play binding (OAuth-based), set up once when Android Enterprise is first enabled in the tenant. Samsung KME and Google zero-touch each have their own separate portal account, outside Intune, that admins maintain independently.

iOS / iPadOS

Two artifacts admins must actively track: the Apple MDM push certificate (renews annually — letting it lapse breaks push notifications to every iOS device in the tenant, not just new enrollments) and the ADE token (.p7m file) from Apple Business, which also has an expiry. Both deserve a calendar reminder well before expiry.

Compliance policies

Android

Compliance policies typically branch by ownership — a stricter device-wide policy for Fully Managed/COPE devices, and a work-profile-scoped policy for BYOD that can only see the container, not the whole phone.

iOS / iPadOS

Similarly split — supervised ADE devices can be checked against device-wide signals; Account-Driven User Enrollment devices are evaluated only on the managed Apple Account partition. Shared iPad has its own caveats, noted in its datasheet above.

Configuration profiles

Android

Settings catalog profiles now cover almost everything custom OMA-URI profiles used to handle for personally-owned work profile devices (with the Wi-Fi PSK exception called out earlier in the legacy section). Corporate-owned modes get the fuller restriction set, including device-wide Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and naming templates.

iOS / iPadOS

Configuration profiles ride on Declarative Device Management by default in 2026, meaning the device proactively reports state changes rather than waiting to be polled — admins will notice compliance and configuration status updating faster than on older protocol versions.

Application deployment

Android

All app delivery flows through Managed Google Play — apps are approved into the enterprise catalog there, then assigned (required/available/uninstall) per group in Intune, same as any other app type.

iOS / iPadOS

Apps are assigned as required/available via Volume Purchase Program-based licensing through Apple Business. One operational detail worth repeating because it trips up so many admins: for ADE devices, Company Portal itself must be deployed as a required app through Intune — never through the public App Store — or automatic updates and full-fleet coverage silently break.

Conditional Access integration

Android

Compliance status from any of the four modes can gate Conditional Access. BYOD work profile compliance reflects only the container's state — Conditional Access policies relying on device-wide signals (like full-disk encryption of the personal side) won't have visibility into that.

iOS / iPadOS

Same principle, with Shared iPad as the standout exception: several Conditional Access condition types (approved client app, app protection policy requirement, per-device terms of use, device-compliance marking, device filters) are explicitly unsupported on Shared iPad — design around that gap rather than discovering it in production.

Reporting and monitoring

Android

Standard Intune device reports cover Fully Managed/COPE/Dedicated cleanly since they're single-user, single-device records. BYOD work profile devices report only the work-container state.

iOS / iPadOS

Shared iPad is the notable gap here too — user-assigned policy reporting doesn't surface cleanly on a device that serves many different people, so plan to lean on sign-in/usage logs from Entra ID rather than device-centric Intune reports if you need per-user audit detail on shared hardware.

§7Common mistakes & key traps

A consolidated list pulled from every card above — the things that actually catch people out in practice (and, often, on certification exams covering this material).

1

Treating "mode" and "method" as the same kind of thing. Comparing Zero-touch to COPE is comparing a delivery truck to a destination.

Fix: Pick the management mode from ownership + personal-use intent first; pick the provisioning method from procurement reality second.

2

Choosing Fully Managed when COPE was actually wanted (or vice versa), discovered only after staff complain they can't add a personal account — or after IT can't wipe a "personal" device they assumed was corporate.

Fix: Confirm the personal-use answer with the business owner before generating the provisioning artifact, not after.

3

Deploying the iOS Company Portal app via the App Store on ADE devices. Breaks automatic updates and isn't supported for automated device enrollment.

Fix: Always deploy Company Portal as a required app through Intune for ADE-enrolled devices.

4

Letting the Apple MDM push certificate or ADE token expire. Silently breaks enrollment and push notifications tenant-wide, often discovered only when a fresh device fails to enroll.

Fix: Calendar both renewal dates well ahead of expiry — don't rely on remembering.

5

Selecting the legacy "Device Admin (Deprecated)" profile type in the Samsung Knox Admin Portal out of habit from an old tutorial — it no longer works on Android 11+.

Fix: Always select the Android Enterprise profile type in Knox for new enrollments.

6

Defaulting BYOD iOS users to "Device Enrollment with Company Portal" or the deprecated profile-based User Enrollment instead of Account-Driven User Enrollment.

Fix: Account-Driven User Enrollment is Apple's current, more private, recommended BYOD path — make it the default policy.

7

Assuming every Conditional Access condition works unchanged on Shared iPad. Several condition types are explicitly unsupported.

Fix: Review the Shared iPad Conditional Access limitations before, not after, designing access policy for ward tablets.

8

Forgetting custom OMA-URI profiles for personally-owned Android work profile devices stopped accepting new policies in April 2025.

Fix: Migrate to settings catalog equivalents; the one carve-out is Wi-Fi PSK, supported through standard Wi-Fi configuration profiles since Q1 2025.

9

Assuming a six-tap QR provisioning gesture will work on a device that isn't truly factory-reset. It silently fails on a device that's already completed Setup Wizard once.

Fix: Confirm a genuine factory reset before attempting QR or NFC provisioning.

10

Leaving devices assigned in Apple Business, the zero-touch portal, or the Knox Admin Portal after retirement or resale. The next owner (or the next internal team) inherits a device that auto-enrolls into the wrong tenant.

Fix: Unassign/release devices from every vendor portal as a mandatory step in the retirement stage of the device lifecycle (Fig. 5, stage 5).

§9Sources & currency

What changed recently, cross-referenced against Microsoft, Google, Samsung, and Apple's own documentation as of June 2026 — and where to check again in six months, because this space doesn't sit still.

Recent changes baked into this guide
  • Apple Business Manager, Apple Business Essentials, and Apple Business Connect were retired and consolidated into Apple Business on April 14, 2026.
  • Android Device Administrator management is fully deprecated for devices with Google Mobile Services, following Microsoft's end of support at the close of 2024.
  • Apple User Enrollment with Company Portal (the profile-based variant) is deprecated and unavailable for new enrollments as of Intune's April 2026 documentation; Account-Driven User Enrollment is the current recommendation.
  • Custom OMA-URI configuration profiles for personally-owned Android work profile devices stopped accepting new policies in April 2025.
  • Declarative Device Management is now Apple's default management protocol across current iOS, iPadOS, and macOS releases.
  • A 2025-reported COPE enrollment issue affects some Samsung Android 16 devices, with Knox Mobile Enrollment documented as the working fallback.
Where to re-check this periodically
  • Microsoft Learn — the Intune "What's new" page, plus the dedicated Android and Apple device-enrollment guide articles.
